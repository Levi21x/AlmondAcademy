The heart is a muscular pump located in the middle mediastinum. It has four chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, and left ventricle. The right side receives deoxygenated blood from the systemic circulation and pumps it to the lungs through the pulmonary artery. The left side receives oxygenated blood from the lungs and pumps it to the body through the aorta. Cardiac valves ensure one-way flow. The tricuspid valve lies between the right atrium and right ventricle, and the mitral valve lies between the left atrium and left ventricle. Semilunar valves include the pulmonary and aortic valves. Coronary arteries supply the myocardium, and coronary perfusion primarily occurs during diastole.

The cardiac cycle describes mechanical and electrical events during one heartbeat. During ventricular diastole, ventricles fill passively after the atrioventricular valves open. Atrial systole contributes a smaller final volume, especially important when ventricular compliance is reduced. Ventricular systole starts with isovolumetric contraction, where pressure rises but volume stays constant because all valves are closed. When ventricular pressure exceeds aortic and pulmonary pressure, semilunar valves open and ejection begins. At the end of systole, isovolumetric relaxation occurs, then ventricular filling restarts. Heart sounds correspond to valve closure: S1 is closure of mitral and tricuspid valves; S2 is closure of aortic and pulmonary valves.

Myocardial infarction is myocardial necrosis caused by prolonged ischemia, usually due to acute thrombotic occlusion of a coronary artery after plaque rupture. Typical symptoms include crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw, diaphoresis, nausea, and dyspnea. Diagnosis combines history, ECG changes, and cardiac biomarkers such as troponin. ST-elevation myocardial infarction requires urgent reperfusion, usually primary PCI, while NSTEMI management includes antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation, statins, and risk-guided invasive evaluation.

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome in which the heart cannot meet metabolic demands without elevated filling pressures. Reduced ejection fraction often reflects systolic dysfunction, while preserved ejection fraction often reflects diastolic dysfunction. Common symptoms are exertional dyspnea, orthopnea, fatigue, and ankle edema. Signs include elevated jugular venous pressure, basal crackles, and peripheral edema. Management includes ACE inhibitors or ARNI, beta blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, and diuretics for congestion.

Arrhythmias are disorders of impulse formation or conduction. Atrial fibrillation causes an irregularly irregular rhythm and increases stroke risk from left atrial thrombus. Supraventricular tachycardia often presents with sudden palpitations and narrow QRS tachycardia. Ventricular tachycardia can cause hemodynamic instability and may degenerate into ventricular fibrillation. Bradyarrhythmias can result from sinus node dysfunction or atrioventricular block and may require pacing.

Basic ECG interpretation starts with rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, and waveform morphology. PR interval evaluates AV conduction. QRS duration indicates ventricular depolarization spread. ST segment and T wave changes help identify ischemia, injury, electrolyte disturbances, and repolarization abnormalities.

Common cardiac drugs include aspirin, which irreversibly inhibits platelet COX-1 to reduce thromboxane A2; P2Y12 inhibitors, which block ADP-mediated platelet activation; beta blockers, which reduce heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand; nitrates, which increase nitric oxide and venodilation to decrease preload; ACE inhibitors, which reduce afterload and remodeling; loop diuretics, which reduce volume overload; and statins, which reduce LDL and stabilize atherosclerotic plaque.
